What to Consider Before Starting a Business?

  • What is a Founders Agreement: A founders agreement is a legal document that spells out how the people starting a company will work together — what each person brings, what they get, and how decisions will be made. Think of it as the friendship insurance policy for co-founders: it keeps enthusiasm from turning into conflict once money, equity, and responsibility enter the picture.
  • Why it’s necessary: When people start a company, they’re usually excited and optimistic — which is exactly why they avoid tough conversations. A founders agreement forces those conversations early, before emotions and money get in the way. It:
    • Prevents misunderstandings later about ownership, effort, or decision-making.
    • Protects the business if a founder leaves suddenly.
    • Provides clarity to investors, who will want to see that the founding team is organized and professional.
    • Reduces the risk of legal battles that can destroy an early-stage business.
  • What it includes:
  • Equity ownership: Who owns what percentage of the business? This reflects initial contributions (money, ideas, time, contacts, technology).
  • Roles and responsibilities: Who does what? For example, one founder handles product development, another handles marketing, another operations.
  • Decision-making: How will big decisions be made? By vote, consensus, or specific roles having authority?
  • Vesting schedule: Founders usually earn their shares over time (e.g., 4 years with a 1-year “cliff” – which is a buffer period when no shares are earned by the founders), so if someone leaves early, they don’t walk away with a huge stake. For example, in a typical 4 year vesting schedule with a 1-year cliff, the founder will receive no shares until the 1-year mark. After the 1-year mark, they will receive 25% of their shares allocated to them. After that, they may earn 2.1% of their shares each month.
  • Intellectual property (IP): Anything created for the purposes of the business — code, designs, trademarks — should belong to the business, not the founders. Without this protection for IP, one of the founders may own the IP for themselves.
  • Capital contributions: Who’s putting in money, equipment, or other resources at the start?
  • Founder departures: What happens if someone quits, is fired, or wants to sell their shares?
  • Confidentiality and non-compete clauses: This is to ensure that any non-publicly available information related to the business may be protected and prevent leaving founder from starting a new business using the ideas of the current business.
  • Dispute resolution: If disagreements arise, how will they be resolved — In court or out of court (Mediation, Arbitration)? Which laws will be applicable? In which country will the dispute be heard?
  • How it relates to later documents: Once the company is incorporated, the founders agreement may evolve into or complement:
    • A shareholders agreement, which governs relationships between all shareholders (not just founders).
    • Articles of association or bylaws, which define the company's structure and governance.
    • The founders agreement lays the groundwork for these, setting expectations before outside investors or new team members complicate the picture.
